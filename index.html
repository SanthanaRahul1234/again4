Everything you need to know about HPV and Cervical Cancer

What is HPV (Human papillomavirus)

empty text

Human papillomavirus (HPV) is the name of a common virus group. They do not cause any problems in most people, but some types can cause genital warts or cancer. Most people who have it do not realise and do not have any problems because HPV does not have any symptoms. But sometimes the virus can cause painless growths or lumps around your vagina, penis, or anus (genital warts).

empty text

Many types of HPV affect the mouth, throat, or genital area. They are easy to catch. You can get HPV from:

  • Any skin-to-skin contact of the genital area

  • Vaginal, anal, or oral sex

  • Sharing sex toys

Most people will get some type of HPV in their life. You do not have to have sexual contact with a lot of people to get HPV. You can get HPV the first time you have sex, but most of the time HPV does not cause any problems. In some cases, HPV can cause:

  • Genital warts

  • Abnormal changes in the cells that can sometimes turn into cancer

  • HPV types linked to cancer are called high-risk types.

  • Cancers linked to high-risk HPV include:

  • Cervical cancer

  • Anal cancer

  • Penile cancer

  • Vulval cancer

  • Vaginal cancer

  • Some types of head and neck cancer

empty text
You can have HPV for many years without it causing problems. You can have it even if you have not been sexually active or had a new partner for many years.

What is Cervical Cancer? And how is it linked to HPV?

empty text

Cervical cancer is the fourth most common cancer in women globally with an estimated 604 000 new cases and 342 000 deaths in 2020.The highest rates of cervical cancer incidence and mortality are in low- and middle-income countries.

empty text

It is a cancer that is found anywhere in the cervix. It mostly affects women under the age of 45. The virus can also cause head and neck cancer, and cancer in other parts of the body. All cervical cancers are caused by certain types of HPV.

empty text

In general, HPV is thought to handle more than 90% of anal and cervical cancers, about 70% of vaginal and vulvar cancers, and 60% of penile cancers. Cancers in the back of the throat (oropharynx) traditionally have been caused by tobacco and alcohol, but recent studies1 2 3 show that about 60% to 70% of cancers of the oropharynx may be linked to HPV. Many of these may be caused by a combination of tobacco, alcohol, and HPV.

More than 90% of all cervical cancer cases are caused directly by HPV. For example, if there were 10,000 patients (about the seating capacity of Cameron basketball stadium at Duke University) diagnosed with cervical cancer about 9000 of these patients would have developed it due to an HPV infection. The rest of the patients may have caught it due to smoking or being chronically immunocompromised which can increase the risk of developing cervical cancer.

empty text
HPV types are often referred to as “non-oncogenic” (wart-causing) or “oncogenic” (cancer-causing), based on whether they put a person at risk for cancer. The International Agency for Research on Cancer found that 13 HPV types can cause cervical cancer, and at least one of these types can cause cancers of the vulva, vagina, penis, anus, and certain head and neck cancers (specifically, the oropharynx, which includes the back of the throat, base of the tongue and tonsils). The types of HPV that can cause genital warts are different from the types that can cause cancer.
empty text
Also, not having routine cervical cancer screening tests (Pap tests) can mean pre-cancers are not found in time to prevent cancer. Chronic immunosuppression, such as from HIV/AIDS or other disorders of the immune system, is also associated with a higher risk of developing cervical cancer. Cervical cancer can be cured if diagnosed at an early stage and treated promptly.

What is the HPV Vaccine?

empty text
So what is it?
The HPV vaccine protects against genital warts and most cases of cervical cancer. It protects against cancer of the vagina, vulva, penis or anus caused by HPV. It also protects against the mouth, throat, head and neck cancers caused by HPV.
empty text
How is it given?
It is given as an injection into your arm. The number of doses needed will depend on your age and how well your immune system works. People under the age of 25 will usually need only one dose whereas people aged 25-45 will need 2 doses. Those with a weakened immune system will need 3 doses.
empty text
How do I get the HPV vaccine?
Children aged 12-13 can get the vaccine at school or for those who missed it can get it at community clinics. It is also available for free on the NHS for all girls aged under 25 and boys born after 1 September 2006. You can check with your school nurse, school vaccination team or GP surgery.
empty text
How well does the HPV vaccine works?
The HPV vaccine works extremely well as it has the potential to prevent more than 90% of HPV attributable cancers. HPV vaccination was first recommended in 2006 and since then, infections with HPV types have dropped 88% among teen girls and 81% among young adult women. Fewer teens and young adults are getting genital warts due to the HPV vaccination. Among vaccinated women, the percentage of cervical pre-cancers has dropped by 40 percent. The protection provided by HPV vaccines lasts a long time. People who received HPV vaccines were examined for 12 years, and their protection against HPV has remained high with no evidence of decreasing over time.
empty text
What are the different types of the vaccine?
There is the Quadrivalent vaccine also known as Gardasil which protects against 4 types of HPV, which together cause around 70% of cervical cancer cases in the UK. This vaccine is available in the UK to all children aged 11 to 13. The Bivalent vaccine also known as Cervarix and has the equivalent cancer-preventing properties as the Quadrivalent vaccine. There is also the Nonvalent vaccine also known as Gardasil 9 protects against 9 types of HPV.

What is in the HPV Vaccine and what are the side effects?

empty text
Things to know:
  • GARDASIL may not fully protect everyone who gets the vaccine.
  • GARDASIL will not protect against HPV types that you already have.
empty text
Do not get GARDASIL if you have experienced
  • an allergic reaction after getting a dose of GARDASIL
  • a severe allergic reaction to yeast, amorphous aluminum hydroxy phosphate sulfate, polysorbate 80.
empty text
Before getting GARDASIL tell your health care provider if you:
  • are pregnant or planning to get pregnant.
  • have immune problems, like HIV infection, cancer, or you take medicines that affect your immune system.
empty text
How is GARDASIL given? You will need 3 shots given on the following schedule:
  • Dose 1: at a date you and your health care provider choose.
  • Dose 2: 2 months after Dose 1.
  • Dose 3: 6 months after Dose 1.
empty text
What are the possible side effects of GARDASIL?
  • pain, swelling, itching, bruising, and redness at the injection site
  • headache
  • fever
  • dizziness
  • vomiting
  • fainting
empty text
What are the ingredients in GARDASIL?
The ingredients are proteins of HPV Types 6, 11, 16, and 18, amorphous aluminum hydroxy phosphate sulfate, yeast protein, sodium chloride, L-histidine, polysorbate 80, sodium borate, and water for injection.

What are Cervical Screenings?

empty text
So what is it?
  • Screening means testing people for preliminary stages of a disease before they have any symptoms. For screening to be useful the tests need to be reliable at picking up cancers or abnormalities that could lead to cancer. Screening tests are not perfect and have some risks.
  • Cervical screening is a way of preventing cancer. It tests for high-risk HPV viruses. Almost all cases of cervical cancer are linked to high-risk HPV.
empty text
Who can have cervical Screenings?
The NHS cervical screening programme invites women from age 25 to 64 for cervical screening. You get an invitation every 3 to 5 years depending on where you live and your age, and you will need to be registered with a GP to get your screening invitations. If you are over 65 and have never had cervical screening, you can ask your GP for a test if you want one. Cervical screening is also for anyone in this age range with a cervix, such as trans men and non-binary people assigned female at birth. You can talk to your GP about this.
empty text
What happens during Cervical Screening?
During cervical screening, a small sample of cells is taken from your cervix for testing. It's usually done by a female nurse or doctor.
  • You'll need to undress, behind a screen, from the waist down.
  • You'll be given a sheet to put over you.
  • The nurse will ask you to lie back on a bed, usually with your legs bent, feet together and knees apart. Sometimes you may need to change position during the test.
  • They'll gently put a smooth, tube-shaped tool (a speculum) into your vagina. A small amount of lubricant may be used.
  • The nurse will open the speculum so they can see your cervix.
  • Using a soft brush, they'll take a small sample of cells from your cervix.
  • The nurse will close and remove the speculum and leave you to get dressed.
  • The cervical screening test itself should take less than 5 minutes. The whole appointment should take about 10 minutes.
You may have some spotting or light bleeding after your cervical screening test. This is quite common and should go away after a few hours. See a GP if you have bleeding after cervical screening which is heavy or does not stop after a few hours

Common Myths about HPV and Cervical Cancer

empty text
Myth #1: People with HPV show symptoms.
Most people with HPV don’t know they’re infected and never develop symptoms or health problems from it. In 90% of cases, your immune system fights off the infection within two years.
empty text
Myth #2: The HPV vaccine has dangerous side effects.
The HPV vaccine known as Gardasil 9 was found to be safe and effective. Like most vaccines, it may cause mild side effects
empty text
Myth #3: HPV interferes with pregnancy.
In most cases, having HPV does not affect a woman’s ability to become pregnant. If you’re pregnant and have HPV, you can get genital warts or develop abnormal cell changes on your cervix. Regular screening can find these, and your doctor can treat them. Becoming pregnant after receiving the HPV vaccine is safe. The vaccine does not affect a fetus.
empty text
Myth #4: Cervical cancer is hereditary:
Cervical cancer is not hereditary like breast cancer and ovarian cancer. It is caused by HPV infection. To save your child from the infection, make sure they get the HPV vaccine. If you are too old to get HPV vaccine, then make sure you get regular HPV and Pap tests.
empty text
Myth #5: HPV infections can be cured.
There is no treatment or cure for the virus itself however many of the conditions that are caused by HPV can be treated. For example, genital warts can be treated by applying prescription medications.
empty text
Myth #6: I have not been sexually active for a long time so I can’t get cervical cancer.
Even if you and your partner have not had intercourse for an exceptionally long time, cervical cancer can still develop.
empty text
Myth #7: I need a Pap Test every year.
Depending on your age and where you live, you will need to take a Pap Test every few years. In the UK, if you are 21-29 years, you will need a test every 3 years; if you are 30-64, this will be 5 years. If you are 65+, you should speak with you gp about whether you need to continue.
empty text

“It’s been incredible to see the impact of HPV vaccination and now we can prove it prevented hundreds of women from developing cancer in England. We've known for many years that HPV vaccination is very effective in preventing strains of the virus, but to see the real-life impact of the vaccine has been truly rewarding. Assuming most people continue to get the HPV vaccine and go for screening, cervical cancer will become a rare disease."

Professor Peter Sasieni, lead study author, from King’s College London,

“I thought that we might be reducing cases of cancer by 50%. And then to see over 80% reduction is just stunning.”

Peter Sasieni

Many people think the vaccine is not safe or unnecessary and this is because sometimes there is false information about HPV vaccines and cervical cancer found on social media. And research has shown that parents who are exposed to misinformation about HPV vaccines on social media are less likely to vaccinate their children.

How to Book for a Cervical Screening Session

empty text

You'll be sent an invitation letter in the post when it's time to book your cervical screening appointment. Your invitation letter will tell you where you can go for cervical screening and how to book. Most cervical screening is done in a GP surgery by a female nurse or doctor. Call your GP to book an appointment with them. You might be able to book the appointment online.

It's best to book an appointment for a time when:

  • you're not having a period – also try to avoid the 2 days before or after you bleed (if you do not have periods, you can book any time)
  • you've finished treatment if you have unusual vaginal discharge or a pelvic infection
